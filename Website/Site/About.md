# What Makes Velocity Different

Velocity is built for low-math, frictionless, story-driven play. It's not a combat simulator or a tactical wargame — combat is a punctuation mark in a scene, not the point of it. Every rule exists to keep romantic tension, supernatural horror, noir mystery, and desperate chases moving, and to get out of the way when it isn't needed.

## Less math, by design.

Velocity actively removes math that doesn't change what happens at the table. Wherever another system asks for a calculation that exists only to justify its own bookkeeping — converting pounds into encumbrance, totaling coin weight, tracking a second resource pool just for spells — Velocity asks whether that number actually changes a scene. If it doesn't, it's gone. That principle shows up everywhere below: in how inventory works, how armor works, even in how dying — and magic — work.

## No classes. No levels.

Characters grow horizontally through Skill Points — discrete investments in exactly the capabilities they've demonstrated, earned, or been changed by. There's no level-up screen, no predetermined advancement path, and no class deciding who a character is before play begins. Identity is earned at the table, not selected from a menu.

## One resolution mechanic.

Every contested action — attacks, persuasion, medicine, tracking, intimidation — resolves the same way: an Opposition Check. Both sides assemble a dice pool and add an attribute bonus. Highest total wins. Once a player understands Opposition Checks, they understand how the entire game works. There's no separate subsystem for combat versus social versus skill checks to keep track of.

## The Narrator, not the GM.

The person running the game is the Narrator. They don't referee rules; they direct scenes — deciding what the world does, how it reacts, and when the camera cuts. It's not a semantic difference. It reflects how Velocity asks the whole table to approach play: lead with the fiction, let mechanics serve the moment instead of gating it.

## Lineage can be earned, not just chosen.

The standard Player's Guide lineages — and the default campaign setting, *The Lost Realms of Anar* — are all available at character creation: pick one and start playing. But lineage is a skill tree, not a fixed stat block, and a setting can choose to gate one behind the story instead. In *Veilborn*, a character doesn't begin the game as a vampire or a werewolf — they become one, through events at the table, not a menu at creation. Same underlying system either way; it's the setting's choice of tone, not a rule every table has to follow.

## Inventory without the spreadsheet.

Carrying capacity is a slot count, not a logistics simulation. Every character gets 50 slots, plus 3 more per point of Strength; items are sized Tiny, Small, Medium, or Large, and cost 1, 1, 2, or 3 slots accordingly. No weight in pounds, no coin-counting, no separate carrying-capacity stat to track — just a number anyone can do in their head.

## Dying is a process, not a coin flip.

Health and Vitality are separate: Vitality sets the ceiling, Health tracks what's left of it. Dropping below 15% of Vitality doesn't mean instant unconsciousness or death — it means the dying state: Consciousness Checks, a ticking Bleeding Out clock, and a real window to stabilize someone before it's too late. Stabilization stops the bleeding but doesn't wake them back up; recovering consciousness is its own, separate act. Death is still death — reaching 0 Health is final, with no death saves — but everything leading up to it is built to create urgency and a real decision point, not a quiet failed roll.

## Armor protects you — it doesn't make you harder to hit.

Most attacks in Velocity connect. Armor doesn't change whether a hit lands; it changes whether the hit matters, absorbing or deflecting damage after the fact instead of subtracting from an attacker's chance to hit. It's the difference between a dragon that survives because everything keeps "missing" it, and a dragon that gets hit, shrugs the damage off, and keeps coming — like soldiers firing on a kaiju that simply doesn't notice.

## Magic costs blood, not mana.

Casting a spell hurts the caster directly: every spell costs Health equal to its level — a 3rd-level spell costs 3 Health, a 9th-level spell costs 9 — and that price never drops, no matter how much a character invests in magic. There's no separate mana pool to track; Health already measures how close to death a character is, so it does double duty as the magic resource too. Cast something powerful enough, often enough, and there's a second cost: the kind of attention a caster really doesn't want to draw.

> "Magic isn't a silver bullet. There are almost always far easier and safer ways to do something without magic than there are with."
> — Rasgard, Wavebinder of Anar

## Built to feel familiar, not to start from zero.

Spell levels, weapon categories, and a straightforward path for converting existing d20-style monsters are all intentionally compatible with material players and Narrators may already know. Velocity isn't trying to make you forget everything you've learned running other systems — it's trying to spend your group's energy on the parts that actually make a scene feel alive, not on rebuilding a bestiary from scratch.
