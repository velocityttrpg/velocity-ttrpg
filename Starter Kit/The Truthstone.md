# The Truthstone

## What It Is

A Truthstone is a civic instrument — not a magic item in the adventurer's sense, but a piece of standing infrastructure, as ordinary and as important as a scale in a market or a seal on a charter. Most tribunal halls in Anar keep one.

The stone itself is roughly the size of a closed fist, dark — deep grey-black, like river stone that has been worn long enough to lose all its edges. Its surface is covered in Faerie runes, fine enough that they are easy to miss in poor light. It sits in a shallow iron cradle on the witness stand.

Only Faeries can make them. This is not a matter of craft or recipe — it is a matter of what Faeries are. The runes are not decorative; they are the stone's function, inscribed by a hand incapable of falsehood into material that remembers it. No human artifice has ever produced one. No human artifice ever will.

*Core Rules reference: Core Rules/Lineages/Faerie.md — Incapable of Deception, Sense Truth.*

## How It Works

A witness places their bare hand on the stone and gives their testimony.

The stone glows **blue** if the testimony is true. It glows **red** if the witness is lying.

It does not distinguish between a sincere belief and a fact. A witness who genuinely believes a false thing will not trigger the red. This is a known limitation, and tribunal law accounts for it — a Truthstone confirms what a witness *believes* they are saying, not what is objectively true. It is a tool for detecting deliberate deception, not for settling disputed facts.

The stone does not glow if not in use. It is cold and dark the rest of the time.

## Its Relationship to Faerie Truthtellers

The Truthstone operates **in the absence of a Faerie Truthteller.** When a Truthteller is present, the stone is not used — a Faerie cannot lie, and can sense deception within ten feet of a speaker. Her presence is its own guarantee.

The stone exists for when no Truthteller is available: when the nearest Faerie is far away, when a Truthteller has been called to a different tribunal, when a community is too small to have one in residence. It is the fallback, not the preference.

In Dunmore, both were available. That is what Voss had to eliminate.

## Its Importance to This Story

The absence of the Truthstone from Kalen Thorne's tribunal is, on its face, the lesser irregularity. Truthstones go missing. They are old objects, sometimes misplaced, sometimes borrowed by other courts. Holt's decision to proceed without it is defensible — inconvenient, irregular, but not unheard of.

The absence of Liriel is what is alarming. Truthtellers do not fail to appear. They do not sleep late or forget. Liriel has attended every tribunal in Dunmore for thirty years.

Voss took the stone first because it was easier to explain. He took Liriel second because the stone alone was not enough.

When the players find the Truthstone in the Counting House subcellar — with Liriel — they have found the proof that both were deliberately removed. That is not a coincidence. That is a conspiracy.

## Describing the Stone

When players first encounter it — either by its absence in Act 1, or by finding it in the Counting House subcellar — the Narrator can describe it as follows:

> *A smooth dark stone, fist-sized, sitting in a shallow iron cradle. Ordinary-looking. The kind of thing you might pick up on a riverbank and not think twice about. Cold to the touch, and completely dark.*

If a witness places their hand on it — in whatever context that occurs — the Narrator describes the glow simply: cold blue light from within the stone, like something held up to a winter window. Or red, if the witness is lying: a warmer, uglier colour, the kind that makes people look away.

The stone does not hum. It does not pulse. It simply illuminates, and everyone in the room can see it.
