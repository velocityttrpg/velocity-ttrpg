# Dunmore and Dardan

*This document is for the Narrator only. It provides the political and social context the Narrator needs to make the world feel real — to answer player questions, set the right tone, and understand why the stakes of this adventure are as high as they are.*

---

## Dardan

Dunmore is a fiefdom within the Kingdom of Dardan. So is Holwick, the town that gives the Holwick Spice Company its name. Everything that happens in *The Weight of Silence* happens on Dardani soil, under Dardani law, and within reach of Dardani justice.

Dardan is ruled by a King and Queen who have governed with particular ferocity on one point: the slave trade is not tolerated. Not fined. Not expelled. Destroyed.

The King in particular has made it a personal crusade. Slavers discovered operating in Dardan face a simple choice: execution on site, or hard labor for the remainder of their natural lives. Their assets are seized in full. Those who were sold are found, freed, and given a portion of the seized wealth to begin new lives. The King does not posture about this. He has done it. He will do it again.

This is not a secret. Every merchant of consequence in Dardan knows it. Every trading company with ambitions in the region knows it. Voss knew it when he agreed to the deal.

**What this means for the adventure:** Voss is not protecting a business deal. He is protecting his life. The contract he is rushing to sign does not just make him rich — it makes him complicit in a slaving operation, on Dardani soil, under the eyes of a King who executes slavers. Dardan is also where he intends to retire. He has been telling himself the secret will hold. He is wrong, and somewhere beneath his composure, he knows it.

The Holwick Spice Company is equally aware of the King's stance. Their expansion into Dardan via the merger is an act of institutional audacity, dressed in legitimacy. The Bank of Belar's seal is not just a formality to them — it is their protection. A Belar-sealed contract, signed by a sitting magistrate of a Dardani township, is the closest thing to armor they can buy.

## Belar by the Sea

The Bank of Belar — commonly called Belar by the Sea — is a sovereign kingdom whose capital, Erevel, sits on a peninsula at the western edge of the known world. Belar does not raise armies or claim territory. Its power is its reputation: for neutrality, for mathematical precision, and for a word that cannot be bought or broken.

Belar sets the world's weights, measures, and currency standards. It oversees international contracts and dispatches banking delegations to witness transactions of significant financial weight. Its seal on a contract means the figures are accurate, the parties are who they claim to be, and the terms were freely agreed to. Without that assurance, international commerce would be slower, messier, and more violent.

Belar holds this reputation to be sacred. Tarnishing it is not merely offensive — it is catastrophic. Fortunes have been destroyed for attempting it. Institutions that have smeared Belar's credibility have found their credit revoked, their contracts voided, and their names removed from the ledgers that matter. Belar does not threaten. It simply remembers, and then it acts, and the action is irrevocable.

The delegation in Dunmore does not know they are about to seal a slaving operation. They believe they are witnessing a legitimate merger between two Dardani spice merchants. If they learn the truth, they will halt proceedings immediately — not out of compassion, but because allowing their seal to appear on a fraudulent, criminal transaction would be the single worst thing that could happen to the Bank of Belar. Their neutrality is their entire institutional identity. They will not risk it for anyone.

**What this means for the adventure:** The players have a second path to stopping the conspiracy that has nothing to do with the tribunal. If they can present credible evidence of the fraud to the Belar delegation — documentation, testimony, the Truthstone — Belar will pull out of the signing. Without their seal, the deal collapses. Holwick loses its legitimacy. Voss loses his exit.

## Shapeshifters and Humans

Shapeshifters and humans share the world uneasily. The distrust is mutual, but it runs from different roots.

Shapeshifters spend their lives in close relationship with the natural world — its rhythms, its health, its balance. They find humans careless and reckless: cities that swallow forests, rivers rerouted for commerce, land stripped faster than it can recover. To a Shapeshifter, humans are not cruel so much as oblivious, which is worse. Cruelty can be reasoned with. Obliviousness cannot.

Humans distrust Shapeshifters for their dual nature — something that looks like a person but is not entirely a person, that can become something else entirely. There is an old, inarticulate unease about this. But what most humans will tell you, if pressed, is simpler: Shapeshifters look at them with contempt. The long, measured looks Shapeshifters give humans when they do something thoughtless — which is often — do not go unnoticed. Humans do not like being found wanting by something that turns into a wolf.

The result is a chronic low-level tension that flares in close quarters and dies down in distance.

**What this means for the adventure:** Kalen Thorne is on trial. He is also a Shapeshifter. For some people in Dunmore, these facts are related in ways they won't say out loud. The prejudice does not excuse their silence — and most of them know it — but it makes the silence easier to maintain. When players canvas the town, they may find people who know something but won't say it, not because they fear Voss, but because the man in the dock is not quite one of them.

The Narrator should let this breathe without editorializing. The discomfort belongs to the characters, not the narration.
