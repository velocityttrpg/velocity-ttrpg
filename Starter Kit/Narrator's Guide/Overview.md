# Overview: The Weight of Silence

## Synopsis

A man stands trial in Dunmore's tribunal hall — accused of consorting with the town's enemies, assaulting a man without provocation, and betraying civic secrets. His name is Kalen Thorne, and the players know him.

The trial should not be happening. By custom and law, a Truthteller — a Faerie whose lineage carries the gift of Sense Truth — must be present at all tribunal hearings. The Truthteller is missing. So is the Truthstone. The chief advocate is pressing forward anyway.

Kalen looks at the players and asks them to tell the court what they saw.

What follows is an investigation into a conspiracy that reaches from Dunmore's tribunal hall to the vaults of the Bank of Belar — and a race against time to save an innocent Faerie's life before the sentence is carried out.

## Themes

- Justice without truth
- Institutional corruption
- The cost of ambition
- Loyalty under pressure

## Structure

The adventure runs in three acts:

**Act 1 — The Tribunal.** The players are called as witnesses in an irregular trial. Their testimony buys time but does not stop the proceedings. The Narrator sets a recess before the verdict is read. The clock starts.

**Act 2 — The Investigation.** Two parallel threads: find the Truthstone, find the Truthteller. Both threads lead to the same man.

**Act 3 — The Confrontation.** The players expose the conspiracy — ideally in the tribunal hall, in front of everyone. The magistrate does not go quietly.

## What This Adventure Showcases

- **Social and investigation scenes as the primary mode of play.** Combat is the climax, not the through-line.
- **Opposition Checks in practice.** When to roll, when to auto-succeed, how the gate works at the table.
- **Action Points in a short, motivated combat.** Three AP per turn, meaningful choices, combat as punctuation.
- **The ticking clock.** Velocity has no initiative stat and no encounter grids. Urgency comes from story, not mechanics.
- **The Narrator's dramatic judgment.** Several moments in this adventure require the Narrator to make a call. Those moments are flagged and supported with guidance.

## The Truth

The following is for the Narrator only.

**Aldric Voss**, one of the three magistrates sitting on the tribunal, is the head of the **Voss Trading Company** — a continent-wide exotic spice merchant whose routes run by land and sea. He has negotiated a deal to sell his entire supplier network to the **Holwick Spice Company**, backed by the **Bank of Belar**. The merged entity will be rebranded as **The Common & Crown Trading Company**. The contract is being finalized today, witnessed by a Belar banking delegation currently in Dunmore.

Voss intends to take the proceeds and retire to **Dardan**, where he plans to buy his way into noble society and never think about Dunmore again.

**Kalen Thorne** stumbled onto evidence of Voss's arrangement. **Davan Reth** — the Holwick Spice Company's man in Dunmore — confronted Kalen in a tavern and threatened him into silence. Kalen swung first. The charges against him are Reth's invention, with Voss's authority behind them.

The Holwick Spice Company is not only a spice merchant. Hidden within its trade routes — concealed beneath legitimate cargo manifests — are two additional lines of business: narcotics distributed through its common-goods network, and slavery. The slave trade is the uglier and more dangerous of the two. It is also the one that brings the King's justice. A magistrate who knowingly facilitated a deal with a slaving operation faces not ruin but execution. Voss understood this when he agreed to the deal. He agreed anyway.

The Bank of Belar does not know. The delegation in Dunmore believes they are witnessing a legitimate merger of two spice trading companies. Their seal on the resulting Common & Crown Trading Company would give a continent-spanning slave operation the institutional legitimacy of Belar's neutrality. This is what Holwick wants. This is what they are paying for.

Kalen saw Reth meeting with a man known in certain circles as a broker of human cargo. He understood immediately what he was looking at. Reth knew he understood — and moved to silence him the same night.

Voss needed both the **Truthteller** and the **Truthstone** removed before the tribunal convened — and he used different hands for each.

The **Truthstone** is a procedural instrument of the tribunal — not a Faerie artifact, but a civic one. When no Truthteller is present, witnesses place their hand on the stone and give their testimony. It glows blue if they speak truth; red if they lie. Removing it was the easier move: an object, concealable, its absence attributable to misplacement. Voss directed Reth to take it the night before the tribunal.

But the Truthstone only operates in the absence of a Faerie. Faeries cannot lie — it is not a discipline or a custom, it is what they are — and a Faerie who takes the stand speaks truth by nature. More than that: within ten feet of a speaker, Liriel knows whether what they say is true. She would have named every lie in the charges the moment they were spoken.

Remove only the Truthstone, and Liriel still exposes the frame. Remove only Liriel, and the Truthstone still catches lies in witness testimony. Voss needed both gone. Reth handled the stone; Voss personally arranged Liriel's imprisonment.

Liriel is imprisoned in the subcellar of the Voss Trading Company's **Counting House**, where she awaits execution once the contract is signed and Voss is safely out of Dunmore. The Truthstone is with her — Reth left it there after taking it, and Voss saw no reason to move it.

The frame job only holds in the silence between them.

The players witnessed the tavern brawl. They know Kalen was provoked — they just didn’t hear what was said. That ambiguity is what opens the investigation. What they will discover is far worse than a corrupt magistrate protecting a business deal.
