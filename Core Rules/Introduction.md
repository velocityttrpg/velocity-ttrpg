# Introduction

This is the **Core Rules** — the complete mechanical reference for Velocity. It defines every system precisely: attributes, Health, Skill Points, Opposition Checks, turn structure, damage, equipment, and the full universal skill catalog. If a rule needs to resolve at the table, this is the document with the final word on how.

The Core Rules are a reference, not an onboarding document. They assume you already know roughly what Velocity is and are here to look up or learn exactly how something works. A Player's Guide and Narrator's Guide are in development and will accompany the first full campaign setting release.

A few terms recur throughout without re-explanation: the person running the game is the **Narrator**, not the GM; a character's ancestry or origin is their **Lineage**, not their race or species. Every term used across all of Velocity's books is defined in [Glossary](../Glossary/Glossary.md) — consult it whenever a word is unfamiliar.

Campaign settings add their own lineages, magic systems, and lore on top of these Core Rules. They don't replace anything here — they extend it.

## What Kind of Game Is This?

Velocity is built around eight principles. Every rule in this document exists to serve them. Every design decision was tested against them. If something in these rules ever feels wrong at your table, these are the right questions to ask.

1. **Narrative First.** Every rule exists to serve the story. A rule that creates tension belongs. A rule that creates friction without serving a story beat is cut.

2. **The Players Are the Stars.** The story centers on the player characters. NPCs, setting, and conflict exist to create conditions for player characters to act. The Narrator serves this principle; the world does not. Rules are for resolving disputes and uncertainty, not constraining player freedom.

3. **The Narrator Directs.** The Narrator's dramatic judgment is final. Rules provide tools and structure; they do not override narrative judgment. There is no adversarial reading of the rules.

4. **Get Out of the Way.** The system does not restrict what players may attempt. At character creation, no template defines a character's concept. At the table, no rule prohibits an attempt that makes narrative sense. Identity is not assigned; it emerges through play.

5. **Eschew Systems Complexity.** Every mechanic must justify its existence. A new subsystem is warranted only when no existing system can handle the problem. Complexity that does not serve the story is cut. New systems must be as simple as possible.

6. **Ruthlessly Eliminate Unnecessary Math.** Resolution uses dice, addition, and comparison. Multiplication, division, and sustained arithmetic during play are not permitted. Where load-bearing math is unavoidable and no simpler alternative exists, it stays — but that is the exception, not the norm.

7. **Rules Do Exactly What They Say.** A rule does what its description says — no more, no less. No implied abilities, no universal inferences, no emergent effects. If a rule has an effect, it is written down.

8. **Horizontal Progression.** Characters grow in breadth, not power. There are no levels. Opportunity cost is the balance mechanism — every choice to invest in one capability is a choice not to invest in another.

## Where to Start

**New to Velocity?** Start with [Opposition Checks](Encounters/Opposition%20Checks.md) — it's the one mechanic everything else builds on. Then read [Characters](Characters/Attributes.md) to understand what a character is made of, and [Skills](Skills/Skills.md) to understand how characters grow.

**Building a character?** Start with [Characters](Characters/Attributes.md), then [Skills](Skills/Skills.md) for the full skill catalog, then [Equipment](Equipment/Weapons.md) for gear.

**Running an encounter?** Start with [Encounters](Encounters/Encounter%20Structure.md) — turn order, actions, damage, and conditions are all there.

**Coming from D&D?** The biggest difference is [Opposition Checks](Encounters/Opposition%20Checks.md) — there are no fixed DCs, no saving throws, and no attack rolls against AC. Every contested action is an opposed roll. Read that first, then [Skills](Skills/Skills.md) to see how the skill system differs from D&D's feat and proficiency model.

**Looking something up?** The [Glossary](../Glossary/Glossary.md) defines every system term. The [Tables](Tables/) chapter collects all reference tables in one place.
