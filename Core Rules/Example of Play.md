# Example of Play

The following scene illustrates how Velocity plays at the table — how Opposition Checks work, how Action Points are spent, and how the narrative stays in front of the mechanics.

| Stealth | II | +1 to Stealth Opposition Checks |

---

When a player makes a check, they roll their base 1d10, add any flat bonuses from skills and attributes, and compare their total to the Narrator's roll. Highest total wins. A tie is a stalemate — neither side gains the upper hand, and the Narrator narrates forward without declaring a winner. Some skills at higher tiers add a second die to the pool; most early investment adds flat bonuses instead.

## Scene: The Counting House

The party needs information from a smuggler named Voss. He's meeting with an associate in the back room of a counting house. Two guards stand outside the door.

**Narrator:** The guards are bored but alert. One is leaning against the doorframe picking at his nails. The other is watching the street. Mira, you're in the shadow of the awning across the lane. Cael, you're beside her. What do you do?

**Mira's player:** I want to slip across the street while the watchful one is looking away. Can I time it?

**Narrator:** You can try. The street guard glances away every forty seconds or so. Roll Stealth.

**Mira's player:** 1d10 base, plus 3 flat from Stealth I through III. *(rolls)* Fourteen.

**Narrator:** *(rolls)* You ghost across the lane without a sound. You're pressed against the wall beside the door, two feet from the bored guard.

**Cael's player:** I want to walk up to the front door openly — like I belong here — and talk my way past the alert guard.

**Narrator:** Roll Persuasion.

**Cael's player:** 1d10, plus 1 from Influence I, 1 from Persuasion I, 1 from Persuasion II, and 1 from Social. So 1d10 plus 4. *(rolls)* Fifteen.

**Narrator:** *(rolls)* He straightens up, hand drifting toward his belt — then your story lands. He relaxes. "Voss isn't seeing anyone." He's not letting you in, but he believes you have legitimate business.

**Cael's player:** Can I push? Tell him Voss is expecting me personally?

**Narrator:** Another Persuasion check. He's already suspicious — the story is changing. I'll give him a bonus die on defense.

**Cael's player:** *(rolls)* Nine.

**Narrator:** *(rolls)* He doesn't buy it. His eyes narrow. "Wait here." He turns to knock on the door.

**Mira's player:** Not happening. I press the crossbow against the bored guard's ribs — quietly. Can I use that to shut them both down?

**Narrator:** That's Intimidation. What do you have for it?

**Mira's player:** No Intimidation skills. But Intimidating Presence gives me plus 4 from my lineage. So 1d10 plus 4.

**Narrator:** The situation is doing the rest of the work. Roll.

**Mira's player:** *(rolls)* Thirteen.

**Narrator:** *(rolls)* The bored guard goes absolutely still. The street guard turns around and sees his partner's face. The knock never lands.

---

## What This Illustrates

Every action was an Opposition Check. The Narrator named the type of check and rolled the opposition privately. Each player assembled their total — base 1d10, flat bonuses from skills, applicable attribute — and announced it before rolling. Neither side rolled against a fixed number.

Mira's Intimidation check is worth noting: she has no investment in Intimidation skills, but her Elf lineage grants +4 on Intimidation Opposition Checks through Intimidating Presence. The crossbow pressed against the guard's ribs gave the Narrator reason to let that bonus carry the scene. Lineage modifiers are always in play — skill investment amplifies them.

If Cael's second Persuasion total had matched the guard's, the result would have been a stalemate. The Narrator narrates forward; nobody wins, and the scene evolves.

## Scene: Voss Makes a Run for It

The interrogation is going nowhere. Voss shoves the table over and bolts for the back exit. His bodyguard — a heavyset man in leather armor with a shortsword — steps between him and the party.

*The Narrator decides the players act first this round. Mira and Cael choose their own order: Mira goes first.*

**Mira's turn — 3 AP**

**Mira's player:** I want to shoot the bodyguard and step back into the doorframe for cover.

**Narrator:** The shot costs 1 AP. Moving costs another. You'll have 1 AP left. Go.

**Mira's player:** 1d10 plus 4 — Ranged Attack II gives me plus 2 to attack with light weapons, Strength gives me plus 2. *(rolls)* Eighteen.

**Narrator:** *(rolls)* He doesn't get clear. Roll damage.

**Mira's player:** 1d8 plus 2 from Ranged Attack III. *(rolls)* Seven.

**Narrator:** The bolt punches through his sleeve. He staggers — still up, but hurting. You're back in the doorframe. Cael?

**Cael's turn — 3 AP**

**Cael's player:** Can I close with him and attack in the same turn?

**Narrator:** He's already close. Movement costs 1 AP, the attack costs 1. You'll have 1 AP left for a Reaction if you need it.

**Cael's player:** 1d10 plus 3 — Melee Attack II gives me plus 2 to attack with light weapons, Strength gives me plus 1. *(rolls)* Twelve.

**Narrator:** *(rolls)* You win — roll damage.

**Cael's player:** 1d8 plus 2 from Melee Attack III. *(rolls)* Five.

**Narrator:** You drive him into the wall. He's bleeding but upright. His turn.

**Bodyguard's turn — 3 AP**

**Narrator:** He swings at Cael — 1 AP. *(rolls)* How do you defend?

**Cael's player:** I'll Parry. No Parry skills, so 1d10 plus nothing. *(spends his remaining 1 AP as a Reaction, rolls)* Five.

**Narrator:** He gets through. The blade catches you across the ribs — you take 4. He uses his remaining AP to plant himself between you and the back door.

**Round 2 — Mira's turn**

**Mira's player:** Forget the bodyguard. I shoot Voss.

**Narrator:** He's moving fast and half behind the doorframe. Cover gives him a bonus die on defense. Roll.

**Mira's player:** 1d10 plus 4. *(rolls)* Twenty.

**Narrator:** *(rolls)* The bolt clips the doorframe — close enough that splinters cut his cheek. He stops. Raises his hands.

**Narrator:** "All right. All right."

---

## What This Illustrates

Each turn, both players had 3 AP and spent it deliberately. Mira used 2 AP to shoot and reposition — and still had 1 AP left. Cael used 2 AP to close and strike, keeping 1 AP in reserve for a Reaction. Unspent AP doesn't carry over, but holding it gives you options during the other side's turns.

Every attack was an Opposition Check. The Narrator rolled the opposition privately and described the outcome. When an attack hits, the attacker rolls damage and announces the raw total — the Narrator applies mitigation privately and describes the effect. For enemies, that description is narrative: "he staggers," "she goes down," "still standing." For player characters, the Narrator announces the net damage number so players can update their Health.

Both sides wore leather armor (mitigation 3 trained), meaning 3 points came off every hit on either side. Armor is applied after the roll — it never affects whether the attack lands.

Cael's shortsword is a light melee weapon. He's trained with it through Melee Attack I–III and suffers no penalty. If he'd tried to use the bodyguard's axe — a medium weapon — he'd take −4 on the attack Opposition Check until he invests in Melee Attack IV. Weapon training determines access and bonuses by size category, not by the specific weapon.

A character can attack more than once per turn when AP allows. A second attack costs 1 AP but incurs a −2 penalty on that roll; a third costs another AP and another −2 (cumulative). Cael's +3 flat bonus from Melee Attack II and Strength directly offsets that penalty — which is why investing in attack skills matters even for characters who aren't primarily fighters.

## Scene: The Back Door

Voss has his hands up. Then the back door splinters inward. Two enforcers — bigger than the bodyguard — step through. The bodyguard, bleeding but upright, lunges for the axe he dropped.

*The Narrator decides the enforcers act first.*

**Narrator:** One enforcer comes straight for Cael. The bodyguard has eyes on Mira. Voss is backing toward the wall. The axe is on the floor between you and the enforcer. What do you do?

**Cael's player:** I grab the axe and swing.

**Narrator:** It's right there — you've got it, no roll needed. But it's a medium weapon. You're trained with light blades, not this. You'll take −4 on the attack.

**Cael's player:** So 1d10, plus 2 from Melee Attack II, plus 1 from Strength — minus 4 for the axe. That's 1d10 minus 1. *(rolls)* Five.

**Narrator:** *(rolls)* The enforcer sidesteps it almost without looking. The blade bites into the table. He shoves you back and brings his longsword around.

*(rolls)* How do you defend?

**Cael's player:** Parry. 1d10 plus nothing. *(rolls)* Three.

**Narrator:** You take 10.

*(Cael entered this scene with 21 Health. 21 − 10 = 11 — below his dying threshold of 13. He enters the Dying condition.)*

**Cael's player:** That puts me at 11. I'm Dying.

**Narrator:** You're Dying — bleeding out, minus 2 on everything. You can still act, but make a Consciousness Check first. 1d10 plus Willpower, against the damage that hit you. That's a 10.

**Cael's player:** Willpower zero. *(rolls)* Six.

**Narrator:** The floor comes up fast. You're out.

---

*Cael is unconscious and bleeding. Mira has the bodyguard and one enforcer. Voss is edging toward the window.*

**Mira's player:** Bodyguard first — he's closest and he's already hurt.

**Narrator:** Go.

**Mira's player:** 1d10 plus 4. *(rolls)* Sixteen.

**Narrator:** *(rolls)* He's wide open. Roll damage.

**Mira's player:** *(rolls)* Eight.

**Narrator:** He goes down hard. One left — and Voss is nearly at the window.

Then something drops through it ahead of him. Something small: barely three feet tall, dark-skinned, compact. A Kin. She lands between Voss and the frame, takes one look at the room — Cael on the floor, the enforcer still standing, Mira cornered — and raises both hands.

---

*Sela is an unexpected ally, her agenda her own. Her stat block for what follows:*

**Sela** — Kin, Magic Attack III (may cast 2nd-level spells), Willpower 2.  
Magic attack roll: 1d10 + 2 (Magic Attack II) + 2 (Willpower) + 2 (Kin lineage) = 1d10 + 6.  
Casting a 2nd-level spell costs her 2 Health.

**Narrator:** She casts.

*(Physical spell — the enforcer can only Dodge. Sela's attack: 1d10 + 6.)*

**Narrator:** *(rolls both)* His Dodge doesn't reach it.

The force bolt takes him off his feet. He doesn't get up.

Sela wipes the corner of her mouth — a thin line of blood, the price of casting. She looks at Cael, looks at Mira.

"He needs stabilizing." A pause. "I can help with that too."

---

## What This Illustrates

**The Narrator decides what needs a roll.** Grabbing the axe didn't need a check — the outcome wasn't uncertain. The Narrator said "you've got it" and moved on. Rolls happen when the outcome is genuinely in question and when rolling raises the stakes. Pickup off the floor doesn't qualify.

**Untrained weapon use shows up in the fiction first.** The −4 penalty on Cael's axe attack meant the enforcer stepped around it like it was nothing. The number didn't just reduce Cael's total — it explained why the story went that way. When the mechanics fit the fiction this cleanly, no one has to explain why something happened.

**Dying is not the end of your turn.** When Cael hit his dying threshold, he entered the Dying condition — −2 on all Opposition Checks, movement reduced by 5 ft, bleeding out every round. He could still act. The Consciousness Check determined whether he stayed conscious enough to do so. If he'd rolled a 10 or higher, he would have been back on his feet — wounded, impaired, dangerous. The bottom half of your Health bar is a last-stand zone, not a waiting room.

**Going unconscious is a story problem, not an elimination.** Cael failed his Consciousness Check, so he went down. That didn't end his role in the scene — it changed what every other character had to respond to. Mira is fighting under pressure. Sela has a reason to act. An unconscious character is a hook, not a removed piece.

**The Consciousness Check is against the damage that triggered it.** Cael rolled 1d10 + Willpower (0) against 10 — the amount of damage that dropped him past his dying threshold. A character with meaningful Willpower investment, or Magic Attack V's trained resilience, would have had a better chance of staying on their feet.

**Casting costs Health — visibly.** Sela paid 2 Health to cast a 2nd-level force bolt. Not a slot, not a point in a mana bar. Health. The blood on her lip was the cost of that casting, legible to everyone in the room. Magic hurts the person using it. That's always true, always present, and never reduced by skill investment.

**Physical spells can only be Dodged.** The enforcer couldn't Parry or Block a force bolt — only get out of the way. A mind-affecting spell would have been resisted with Willpower instead. The type of defense available is determined by the type of spell, not the defender's preference.

**Sela chose a 2nd-level spell deliberately.** At Magic Attack III she can cast up to 2nd-level spells — and she did. Level 0–2 spells never draw attention regardless of how many are cast. If she had pushed to a 3rd-level spell — which would require Magic Attack IV — the Narrator would begin tracking escalating attention risk: the deeper into the Magesea (or whatever power a setting taps), the more dangerous repeated casting becomes. She used what the situation needed and stayed quiet. That's also a decision characters make at the table.
