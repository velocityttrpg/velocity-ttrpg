# Environmental Hazards

## Overview

Environmental hazards are passive, ongoing threats posed by the environment itself — extreme temperatures, toxic air, oxygen deprivation, and similar conditions that wear a character down over time. They are not combatants and do not roll against the character; instead, the character rolls to resist them.

Environmental hazards are resolved as **Fortitude checks against a static difficulty**, making them one of the system's two deliberate exceptions to the opposed-dice-pool model (alongside Stabilization — see [Opposition Checks](Opposition Checks.md) §4).

## Severity

The Narrator assigns a severity tier based on narrative logic. The same hazard can occupy different tiers depending on circumstances (a cold snap at night is Mild; the same cold snap with wet clothing and no shelter may be Severe).

| Severity | Examples | Check Frequency | Difficulty | Failure Effect |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :---: | :--- |
| **Mild** | Cold snap, dry heat, stale air, high elevation (thin air, breathlessness), stale mine air | Once per scene | 8 | **Hindered** until the exposure ends or the character rests in a safe environment |
| **Severe** | Blizzard, scorching desert, smoke-filled room, very high altitude (altitude sickness), carbon dioxide buildup in enclosed spaces | Once per round | 14 | **1d4 damage + Hindered** |
| **Lethal** | Arctic exposure without shelter, volcanic heat, toxic atmosphere, death-zone altitude, oxygen depletion, hydrogen sulfide or methane gas pockets | Once per round | 20 | **2d8 damage + Hindered** |

## Making the Check

The character rolls their dice pool + Fortitude against the hazard's difficulty. On a result equal to or higher than the difficulty, the check succeeds and the character suffers no effect this interval. On a lower result, apply the failure effect for that severity tier.

Applicable bonuses from the Fortitude skill tree apply normally.

## Mitigation

Appropriate equipment or shelter reduces the effective severity by one tier:

- **Mild** hazard with mitigation: no check required.
- **Severe** hazard with mitigation: treat as Mild.
- **Lethal** hazard with mitigation: treat as Severe.

What counts as mitigation is Narrator judgment based on narrative logic:

- Cold weather clothing and fire mitigate freezing temperatures.
- Shade, water, and appropriate clothing mitigate extreme heat.
- A breathing apparatus or sealed environment mitigates toxic atmosphere and oxygen deprivation.
- Supplemental oxygen or acclimatization time mitigates high-altitude low-oxygen hazards.

Partial mitigation (inadequate gear, improvised shelter) may reduce severity by one tier at the Narrator's discretion, or impose disadvantage on the Fortitude check without changing the tier.

## Wilderness Skills

The Wilderness skill line represents expertise in surviving natural environments. The Narrator may determine that sufficient Wilderness investment (particularly Wilderness IV and above) provides improvised mitigation against environmental hazards encountered in natural settings — building adequate shelter, locating water in heat, and similar field craft.

Wilderness skills do not automatically negate hazards; they give the Narrator grounds to reduce severity when the character takes appropriate action.

## Flammable Atmospheres

Some hazardous environments are not merely toxic — they are flammable. Methane gas pockets in mines and other enclosed spaces are the most common example. Flammable atmospheres have two distinct phases.

**Before ignition**, a flammable gas pocket behaves as a Severe or Lethal toxic atmosphere (breathlessness, disorientation, headache). Characters may not realize the air is flammable rather than merely bad without relevant knowledge or a successful check.

**Detection.** The Narrator may offer environmental cues before or as the party enters: torchlight that gutters, dims, or turns bluish; a faint sulfur or rotten-egg smell; a dead animal near the tunnel entrance; an unusual stillness in the air. A successful Perception or Wilderness check allows a character to notice and identify one such cue. Characters with mining experience or relevant background may recognize the danger immediately at the Narrator's discretion.

**Ignition** occurs the moment an open flame — a torch, a lantern, a fire-based spell, a spark from struck steel — enters a flammable gas pocket. No roll is made. The gas ignites.

The explosion affects all characters within the pocket. The Narrator determines the radius and severity based on the size of the pocket, but as a baseline:

- All characters in the area take **6d10 fire damage** and are knocked **Prone**.
- Characters at the edge of the blast radius take **3d10 fire damage**.
- The tunnel or chamber may suffer structural damage — cave-in risk, collapsed exits, or fire spreading to wooden supports — at the Narrator's discretion.

**Aftermath.** The explosion consumes the available oxygen in the immediate area, leaving it oxygen-depleted — a Lethal hazard with no warning cues, since the methane is gone and only the absence of breathable air remains. Light sources in the blast area are extinguished by the pressure wave or starved of oxygen; the party may find themselves in total darkness. At the periphery, where oxygen wasn't fully consumed, wooden supports and other combustible materials may catch fire, creating a secondary hazard and potential structural collapse.

## Notes

1. Environmental hazards are not combat encounters. They run in the background of scenes and exploration, applying pressure without requiring dedicated turns.
2. If a character is simultaneously exposed to a hazard and in combat, Fortitude checks occur at the start of the character's turn, before AP is spent on other actions.
3. The Narrator should telegraph hazard severity before characters commit to exposure — players should be able to make informed decisions about whether to enter a dangerous environment. Flammable atmospheres are an exception: the Narrator may withhold the flammable nature of the gas while still offering non-specific cues (the torch gutters, the air smells wrong) that reward cautious players who pay attention.
