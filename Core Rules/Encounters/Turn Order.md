# Turn Order

## Overview

1. Turn order determines which side acts first each round, and which participants act when within that side.
2. There is no Initiative roll, attribute, or score. Turn order is resolved in two steps: a single Narrator determination of side order, and ongoing choices by each side about their own acting order.

## Sides

1. For turn order purposes, every participant in an encounter belongs to one of two sides: the players' side, or every other participant.
2. If multiple non-player factions are present (e.g., two rival enemy groups, or hostile and neutral NPCs), they are still treated as a single side for turn order. Their internal priorities are a roleplay and Narrator concern, not a turn-order one.

## Determining Side Order

1. When an encounter begins, the Narrator privately determines which side acts first.
    1. This may be a hidden roll, or a direct ruling based on the fiction — both are valid. The result isn't revealed mechanically; the table simply finds out by seeing who acts first.
    2. The Narrator should weigh which side is more prepared, alert, or positioned to act decisively, but is not required to justify the result.
2. Once determined, side order remains in effect for the rest of the encounter.
    1. The Narrator may call for a new side-order determination if the situation changes dramatically — for example, a new faction enters the fight, or an effect causes one side to lose its footing entirely.
3. Each round, the side that goes first completes all of its turns before the other side begins.

## Acting Order Within a Side

1. A side's participants decide their own acting order at the start of each round.
    1. This decision is made openly, in character or out, as the players (or the Narrator, for NPCs) see fit.
    2. A side may choose a different order each round to respond to how the previous round played out.
2. Once a participant's turn begins, it proceeds normally — they spend their Action Points, take Reactions when eligible, and so on, per [Actions](Actions.md) and [Encounter Structure](Encounter Structure.md).
3. A participant who hasn't acted yet may always choose to go later in their side's order for that round; there is no formal "delay" action required to achieve this. See §6 (Readying an Action) for the one case that does need a formal option — acting on a trigger during the other side's turns.

## Surprise

1. Surprise is a narrative determination, not a roll.
2. If the fiction supports it — an ambush, a trap sprung, a target caught completely unaware — the Narrator may rule that the surprising side acts first for the encounter (overriding §3's normal determination) and, if the surprise is severe enough, that the surprised side cannot act at all during the first round.
3. A surprised side that cannot act during the first round still decides its acting order normally starting in round two.

## Readying an Action

1. Readying is a Free Action that lets a participant act on a trigger during the other side's turns, when their side's chosen order would not otherwise let them act at that moment. This option costs no AP.
2. A participant may ready a single action by specifying a trigger and the action they will take when that trigger occurs.
3. The participant must have enough unspent AP to perform the readied action at the time they declare it.
4. When the trigger occurs — even during the other side's turns — the participant may immediately take the readied action.
5. If the trigger doesn't occur before the round ends, the readied action is lost.

## Simultaneous Actions and Ties

1. When two participants on the same side want to act at the exact same moment (e.g., a coordinated combo), the players involved agree on the order. If they can't agree, the side's order for that round stands.
2. When a Reaction or readied action from one side interrupts the other side's turn, it resolves immediately, before the triggering action completes.
3. If two Reactions from different participants would resolve at the same moment and the order matters, the participant with the higher Reflex score goes first. If still tied, the players involved decide who goes first; if they can't agree, the Narrator decides.

## Exceptions and Special Cases

1. A lineage, skill, or ability may grant a participant the right to act outside their side's normal position — for example, always acting first regardless of side order, or interrupting another participant's turn. Such abilities define their own triggers and limits and override this document where they conflict.
